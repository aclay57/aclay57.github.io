FunCheapSF

Mobile First Redesign

Go to Prototype

PROJECT BRIEF

FunCheapSF started as a passion proejct but needed a professional design perspective based on user research

We were able to work directly with the founder who wanted to emphasize mobile and improve the information architecture and visual design for their web and mobile applications


GOALS

The project goal was to create a mobile first design that would improve information architexture and visual design to make the product clear and accessible.


ROLE

I was able to work with Jorge Maya, a designer from the General Assembly ux immersive course to develop this design over the course of 4 weeks.


Researching Needs

Before doing specific testing with FunCheapSF, we wanted to get a better understanding of how people learn about and share events in general. Specifically we wanted to speak with the client’s target audience for expansion: students and tourists. We visited Fisherman’s Wharf, the Berkeley campus, and Dolores park for some guerrilla user testing.

Testing Status Quo

Understanding the use of the site ‘as is’ was critical to finding pain points. Testers were asked to browse, find an event for the weekend, and share an event with a friend. The most common issue was finding relevant events. Rather than using the filters and categories, many users would use the search bar, which often produced no results or took significantly longer than the filter feature. We also implemented card sorting to determine the most relevant features for actual event pages.

We created a survey using TypeForm and the link was posted on the website. We were able to get 286 results that led to a wealth of insights, favorite features, and recommendations.

Life Alert

Emergency Alert Device Redesign

Go to Prototype

PROJECT BRIEF

Life Alert currently rests on its reputation as the top emergency medical device and needs an update to remain competitive within the market


GOALS

Create a functional, respectful website for Life Alert that better includes loved ones in their services so that their customers are able to have a more enjoyable and efficient experience


ROLE

I worked with two other UX designers, Amanda Richardson and Shawn Huang, from General Assembly over the course of two weeks to complete this project.



Competitive Analysis

Understanding the competitive landscape was key to honing in on Life Alert’s potential to offer a unique service. The main difference with direct competitors was the wide range of services offered. While Life Alert provided a necklace and mobile option, the range was extremely limited and functionality was minimal. Outside services like utility companies were also considered as a model for online ordering of a service that required in-person installation.


Research


In individual interviews were used to determine the needs, pain points, and overall perception associated with Life Alert. We spoke with a nurse, an EMT, three caretakers, and a person already using the device to start. We were surprised to find a lack or correlation between seniors that had medical issues warranting the use of an emergency device and those that were actually accepting of their condition. A divide existed between those who felt that the device was a smart choice and others who felt that such a device indicated a lack of independence. We also noted that many seniors were comfortable online and had experience managing other online accounts such as bank accounts etc.

User Testing


We tested our prototype with a six different seniors. We asked them to explore products, access the network as if they had the service, and their overall impression of the site. We got lots of actionable feedback and were also delighted that one previously skeptical user wanted to purchase the product on the spot.

Designing For Seniors

Creating a product that appealed both to caretakers and senior end users was critical. We took into consideration general principles to make sure the site was accesible.


Personas

After understanding the users we created personas to be sure that they met the needs of a variety of users. The personas were then used to check in throughout the design process to be sure that we were solving problems for real users.
